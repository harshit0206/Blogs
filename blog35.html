A barcode is a machine-readable code that is presented on a product and consists of numbers and a pattern of parallel lines of varied lengths. Businesses and organizations may track items, pricing, and stock levels with barcode systems, resulting in considerable productivity and efficiency advantages. The use of a barcode cuts the time it takes to gather such data in half and eliminates the risk of human data input mistakes.

You can produce several various sorts of barcodes with your Mac, each with its own set of benefits and drawbacks.

1D Barcode type


One-dimensional (or 1D) barcodes encode data by systematically varying the lengths and spacing of parallel lines. In order to reduce time and streamline inventory procedures, 1D barcodes are commonly used in commercial operations.

E.g. UPC, EAN, Code 39, Codabar, Interleaved 2 of 5, and many more.



2D Barcode Type


Two-dimensional (or 2D) barcodes reliably represent data using two-dimensional symbols and shapes. When compared to 1D barcodes, they can contain more data per square inch. Error-protection equations are also available in 2D barcodes, which is a significant benefit. 2D barcodes are well-suited to high-volume, high-speed scanning applications.

E.g. DataMatrix, QR Code, PDF 417.

Barcode Hardware

i. Barcode Label Printers


Thermal printing, which is great for label printing on the Mac, comes in two flavors. For your Mac, you may use either a thermal transfer printer or a direct thermal printer.


a. Direct Thermal Printer

For producing barcode labels on a Mac, direct thermal printers are a cost-effective choice. Because it doesn't need ink, toner, or a printing ribbon, you save money on supplies. Labels should last for as long as possible if they are not exposed to direct sunshine or high heat.

The picture is created directly on the label material, which is heat-sensitive chemically treated paper, using heat from a thermal printer. When the print head's components make contact with direct thermal paper, it turns black. This is the type of printing you'll find on receipts from ATMs, petrol stations, and other businesses.


Label life is only around six months, and direct contact with the label medium causes the printhead to wear out faster. A direct thermal printer is a fantastic alternative to consider if your barcode labels will be used largely inside.


b. Thermal Transfer Printer

Thermal transfer is another heat-based barcode printing method. Instead of instantly imprinting on the paper, the printhead's hot element makes contact with a particular ribbon, which then conveys the image to the label media. The wax or resin of ribbon melts into the label, hardening to a durable, long-lasting gloss.

Thermal transfer uses a ribbon, which wears down the printhead more slowly and, most significantly, provides long-lasting labels.


In tough situations, thermal transfer generates long-lasting labels that resist scratches and smudging.

Among the numerous types of thermal printers for Mac, there are more alternatives in terms of features and functionality. You can make barcodes with a variety of printers, including desktop, mid-range, industrial, and portable thermal barcode printers.


ii. Barcode Scanners


To decode the created barcodes, your Mac has four different types of barcode scanners.

  1. Pen barcode Scanner

    Pen barcode readers are little wand-like sticks that resemble a pen. An LED light and a photodiode are located at the tip of the pen-style barcode reader. When the user moves this tip over a barcode, the LED light illuminates the black and white bars. Each bar's width and color are determined by the photodiode, which detects light reflections.

  2. Laser barcode Scanner

    A laser barcode scanner is a step up from a pen scanner in that it can take more exact light readings, reducing false positives and scanning errors. In a laser scanner, a laser beam is directed at a mirror within the real scanner. Because of the movement of this mirror, the laser sweeps across the barcode in a straight line. This reflection is used to convert the digital signal readout of the barcode.

  3. CCD barcode Scanner

    Hundreds of small LED lights are stacked in a continuous row on a charge coupled device (CCD), often known as an LED scanner. These lights are projected directly onto a barcode, and a sensor in front of each light bulb monitors the voltage of ambient light. This voltage measurement is used to digitally capture the barcode.

  4. Image barcode Scanner

    These work more like a video camera than a scanner. The machine features a video camera that records a picture of the barcode, which is subsequently analyzed using powerful digital decoding algorithms instead of using the laser to shoot light at it. The main advantage of this type is that it can read barcodes no matter which way the scanner is positioned in front of it.


How to connect a scanner & printer with Mac
  • Before you begin setting up your scanner and printer, make sure Mac OS has the latest scanner and printer software.
  • If you have a multifunction AirPrint scanner and printer, you won't need to check for software updates.
  • To unpack and connect your scanner or printer to your Mac, follow the directions that came with it.
  • If your Mac only has one USB-C port and you want to connect a USB printer, you'll need a multiport converter. To increase the battery life of your Mac, connect the scanner or printer and then plug in a USB-C connection to a power supply.
  • Select System Preferences -> Add Printer & Scanner from the Apple menu.
  • Then, after selecting your scanner or printer, click add.
