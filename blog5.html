Barcodes for Publication

Identification of a product and its price can be done by using barcode instead of manually typing letters and numbers as scanning is accurate and faster. Barcodes help bookstores manage the count, the value and the pricing of their inventory. A barcode puts numbers into a graphic form that can be read by a scanner.

Booksellers, universities and distributors are required to use an International Standard Book Number (ISBN) for their books. It is also used by the publishing industry to monitor sales data. Books that are distributed in retail bookstores, libraries, and wholesale companies must have ISBN.

ISBN identification format was approved in 1970. It is a standard that establishes a unique identifier for each publication and edition of a title from a specific publisher.

The ISBN is used internationally and every country has one agency authorized to sell ISBNs to publishers in that country. It is a unique 13-digit number used by the bookstores and book wholesalers as the basis of inventory systems.

The barcode of most books actually consists of two barcodes inside the barcode area: one for the ISBN and one for the retail price. The barcode found on the back of books are called Bookland EAN.

Barcode equivalent of ISBN is called EAN-13 as it contains 13 digits. The EAN barcode is created from the ISBN for the book.

The ISBN-13 took effect on January 1, 2007 which is the extension of the original 10-digit ISBN. The new ISBN-13 code is also known as Bookland EAN

Types of barcode used in publisher industry

Universal Product Code (UPC)

The UPC code is seen on trade and mass-market books, as well as general magazines. The UPC numbering code has twelve digits. It is divided into two sections of variable length.

The first section is known as the Company Prefix. It varies between six and nine digits.

The second section is the Item Reference. The Item Reference varies between five digits and two digits depending on the length of the Company Prefix.

A single check digit completes the code. The UPC does not include the ISSN. The UPC has been adopted throughout general retailing.

International Article Number (EAN)

Another type of bar code is the International Article Number (EAN), a thirteen digit number that was developed from the UPC.

Every 12-digit UPC number can become a 13-digit EAN number by the addition of a leading zero.

The EAN is divided into five parts. Each part is separated by a hyphen. It starts with a 978 prefix, followed by a country identifier, a publisher identifier, a title identifier and closes with a check digit that validates the number.

The number 0 was assigned to the UPC. Thus the 12-digit UPC in the US and Canada can be the 13-digit EAN by the addition of the 0 prefix.

Bookland EAN "add-on"

The retail price barcode (EAN 5) lists retail price as a 5-digit number. If the price is less than $100.00, the first digit of EAN 5 will denote the currency, like 5 is used for $ US. The remaining four digits are the retail price.

Barcode is created by encoding an EAN 13 barcode font type directly with the first 12 digits of the ISBN-13 number. The first 9 digits of ISBN-10 number are appended to 978 for encoding in EAN 13 barcode font type.

The last digit of the ISBN number is a checksum digit, which is represented as number between 0 and 10. When the check digit is equal to 10, the Roman numeral X is used to keep the same amount of digits in the number.

The last digit of the ISBN number is never encoded in the barcode. After encoding the 12 digit number in the EAN 13 barcode, a new EAN 13 check digit is generated for the barcode symbol.
